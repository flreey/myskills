# Writing / Copy Template

## Vague input
> "Help me write something about anxiety."

## Questions to ask (≤3)
1. Who reads it? (general public / people with anxiety / clinicians / your friends / "choose for me" → people with anxiety)
2. Purpose? (reduce shame / explain mechanism / offer practical tactics / "choose for me" → tactics + reduce shame)
3. Length and channel? (tweet thread / 800-word blog / newsletter / "choose for me" → 800-word personal blog)

## Final prompt

```text
Goal:
Write an 800-word personal essay that gives an anxious reader (1) the relief of being understood and (2) two tactics they can try tonight.

Audience:
People who already know they have anxiety. Not seeking diagnosis. Tired of articles that say "try yoga." Want something specific and unjudgmental.

Main thesis:
Anxiety isn't a flaw — it's a calibration problem. You can't switch it off, but you can shift what your nervous system is calibrating to.

Outline:
1. Open with one specific scene (a moment of pre-meeting dread, not a generic feeling). 80 words.
2. Reframe: anxiety is a smoke detector with the gain too high. 150 words.
3. Tactic 1: "Name three things louder than the alarm" — sensory anchor, with one example. 200 words.
4. Tactic 2: "Schedule the worry" — a 10-minute window, why it works. 200 words.
5. Close with what *not* to expect (no cure, just a turn of the dial). 100 words.
6. One-line sign-off. ~70 words.

Tone:
Warm, plainspoken, slightly dry. Like a friend who's been there, not a therapist. Use "you" sparingly. No hedging ("maybe", "perhaps", "might consider").

Examples to anchor tone:
- Mary Karr's clarity
- Anne Lamott's "Bird by Bird" voice (not the spiritual stuff, just the cadence)

Avoid:
- "Just breathe", "you got this", "manifest"
- Stats without source
- Telling the reader they're "brave" for reading this
- Long abstract paragraphs about the brain

Acceptance criteria:
- A reader with anxiety could try one tactic the same night
- No paragraph longer than 5 sentences
- At least one concrete sensory detail per section
- Word count 750-850
```

## Notes
- Outline is the highest-leverage part — without it, "write 800 words" produces sludge.
- Voice references > tone adjectives. "Mary Karr's clarity" beats "be clear."
- Avoid list catches the most common LLM-essay clichés.
